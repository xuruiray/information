China’s Chang’e-6 reveals why solar wind hits the Moon’s two sides differently ScienceDaily 技术

Chang'e-6 samples show solar wind particles penetrate deeper on the Moon's far side because Earth's magnetosphere slows particles reaching the near side. Noble gases in lunar soil record this uneven bombardment, potentially aiding solar system history studies.

Scientific Research Briefing

Astronomers confirm first atmosphere on rocky habitable-zone planet LHS 1140 b; solar 'switch-off' signal predicts storm strength 7 years early; Shimao ancient DNA reveals early East Asian social structure; three new Helicobacter bacteria found in whale stomachs.

Astronomers find the first atmosphere on a rocky world in the habitable zone ScienceDaily 科学

Astronomers confirmed the first atmosphere on a rocky planet, LHS 1140 b, in a star's habitable zone. Located 48 light-years away, its atmosphere was revealed by leaking helium. It may have been preserved for billions of years, making it a prime target in the search for potentially habitable worlds.

Read more → A hidden “switch-off” signal could predict solar storms seven years early ScienceDaily 科学

A newly discovered turning point in the Sun's 11-year cycle, the 'switch-off' signal, allows early prediction of solar activity. After this point, most violent space weather abruptly ends, and remaining sunspots offer clues about the next cycle's strength. Early projections suggest Solar Cycle 26 may be moderate.

Read more → Ancient DNA reveals a chilling secret at China’s 4,000-year-old Shimao city ScienceDaily 科学

Ancient DNA from China's 4,000-year-old Shimao settlement reveals how one of East Asia's earliest complex societies was organized. Its people were largely descended from local Yellow River farming communities but also had genetic ties to distant southern and steppe populations, pointing to extensive prehistoric contact.

Read more → Scientists found mystery bacteria inside one of the ocean’s most elusive whales ScienceDaily 科学

Scientists studying decades of pygmy sperm whale strandings discovered three previously unknown Helicobacter bacteria in their stomachs. All four infected whales showed serious digestive damage, including inflammation, ulcers, scarring, and parasite infestations, though the bacteria were not confirmed as the cause of death.
