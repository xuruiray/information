Scientific Research Briefing

This subsection covers four research findings: Webb telescope's discovery of exoplanet Beta Pictoris d via atmospheric traces; NASA's orbital rehearsal for Moon lander docking; orca's high-speed impact behavior on sunfish; and neural switches in bee brains that control task transition. These studies enhance our understanding of the universe and animal behavior.

NASA will test SpaceX and Blue Origin Moon landers before the next lunar landing ScienceDaily 科学

NASA is planning an ambitious orbital rehearsal for future Moon landings. During the Artemis III mission in 2027, Orion astronauts will separately rendezvous and dock with prototype lunar landers from Blue Origin and SpaceX. This test will allow crews and ground teams to verify complex maneuvers necessary for later crewed surface missions, ensuring safe and successful landings.

Read more → NASA’s Webb telescope discovers giant planet hiding in one of astronomy’s most famous systems ScienceDaily 科学

A giant planet had been hiding inside one of astronomy's most studied planetary systems, concealed by a bright disk of cosmic dust. Webb's detection of carbon monoxide, water vapor, and methane in the atmosphere revealed Beta Pictoris d, showcasing a promising new method for finding elusive exoplanets that are otherwise invisible.

Read more → Orcas filmed smashing giant sunfish into thousands of pieces ScienceDaily 科学

Researchers in the Gulf of California documented a dramatic orca behavior never described before. In two separate encounters, one orca gripped a dead sunfish by the fin while another charged into it at high speed, blasting the carcass into thousands of pieces. This discovery reveals a complex cooperative feeding strategy.

Read more → Scientists discover the hidden brain switch that assigns bees their jobs ScienceDaily 科学

Researchers found that specific brain circuits help decide which jobs bees perform as they age. When those circuits were switched off, older worker bees began caring for the queen again instead of carrying out their normal later-life duties. The findings reveal that a bee colony's complex division of labor may be guided by neural switches hidden inside each bee.
